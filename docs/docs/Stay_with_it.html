Changing winds 💨
An over-the-counter trading firm, B2C2, has received an approval from U.K.'s Financial Conduct Authority (FCA) to offer crypto derivatives, namely contracts for difference (CFDs), in the country. 🤝 This approval is notable because the FCA, which is the financial watchdog of the U.K., has previously issued warnings over crypto CFDs. 

CFDs allow traders to speculate on the future price changes of specific cryptos, and potentially profit by going long or short. B2C2's CFD product caters to Bitcoin (BTC), Bitcoin cash (BCH), Ether (ETH), Litecoin (LTC) and XRP (XRP). 

According to the founder of B2C2, Max Boonen, this CFD product offering will allow clients to "gain derivative exposure to the cryptocurrency markets” while avoiding the “risks associated with crypto custody.” 👌🏻
Hmmm 🤔
After the death of its founder Gerry Cotten on Dec 9, crypto exchange QuadrigaCX has been unable to access ~$145 million of its digital assets, leading to a liquidity crisis. 💸

QuadrigaCX kept most of its assets in cold wallets, which are secured by digital security keys. However, Cotten was the person solely responsible for the wallets and keys. 🤦‍♂️ Cotten's wife, Jennifer Robertson, reportedly stated in an affidavit, “I do not know the password or recovery key. Despite repeated and diligent searches, I have not been able to find them written down anywhere.”

However, QuadrigaCX users remain highly skeptical about all this, as evidenced by its subreddit channel. Cotten supposedly died at 30 from Crohn's disease (usually not fatal), while in India building an orphanage. His statement of death is not an official death certificate and comes from a funeral home, one that some QuadrigaCX users attempted to call to no avail. Additionally, Cotten was in India at a time when he was in the middle of a court battle with the Canadian Imperial Bank of Commerce (CBIC), and signed his will less than two weeks before he died. 🤔
Beware malware 🛡️
According to a report by cybersecurity firm Palo Alto Networks, there is a new form of malware that targets Apple Mac computers such that it intercepts and steals browser cookies information related to crypto exchanges and wallet service providers’ websites visited by the victims.

The "CookieMiner" malware targets sites like Binance, Coinbase, Poloniex, Bittrex, Bitstamp amd MyEtherWallet, and domains with the word “blockchain” in them. It attempts to steal credit card information, saved usernames and passwords in Chrome, iPhone text messages that are backed up to iTunes and crypto wallet keys, which it collectively uses to navigate past the authentication processes of crypto exchanges. 😠

The malware also changes the victims' system configuration to maliciously load crypto mining software to mine a crypto called "Koto". According to Palo Alto Networks, the firewall program called "Little Snitch" prevents CookieMiner from working. 
Blockchain term of the day 🤓 - Lightning Network

The Lightning Network is a "second layer" payment protocol that operates on top of a blockchain. Theoretically, it will enable fast, scalable transactions between and across participating nodes, and has been touted as a solution to the Bitcoin scalability problem.
